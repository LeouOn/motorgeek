## 1. Current California Smog Framework

### 1.1 The Biennial Smog Check Program

California's Smog Check Program, administered by the Bureau of Automotive Repair (BAR) under the Department of Consumer Affairs, requires emissions inspection for most gasoline vehicles 1976 model year and newer. The program divides the state into three areas:

| Area Type | Counties | Biennial Required? | Change of Ownership? |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Enhanced** | Los Angeles, Orange, Riverside, San Bernardino, etc. | Yes | Yes |
| **Basic** | Sacramento, Fresno, Sonoma, etc. | Yes | Yes |
| **Change of Ownership** | Rural counties (Modoc, Lassen, etc.) | No | Yes |

### 1.2 OBD2 Testing for 2000+ Vehicles

Vehicles model year 2000 and newer receive an **OBD2-focused inspection** (officially called the OIS — OBD Inspection System):

- **No tailpipe testing** — the sniffer is retired for 2000+ vehicles
- Inspector plugs into the OBD2 port and reads:
  1. Malfunction Indicator Lamp (MIL) status — must be off
  2. Diagnostic Trouble Codes (DTCs) — none stored or permanent
  3. Readiness monitors — all must be set (as of October 2025, BAR removed previous exemptions for hard-to-set monitors)
  4. Visual inspection for tampered/missing emissions equipment (including catalytic converters)
  5. Gas cap functional test
  6. Visual check for liquid fuel leaks

### 1.3 Exemptions (Current Law)

| Vehicle Category | Biennial Exempt? | Transfer Exempt? |
|---|---|---|
| 1975 model year and older | Yes | Yes |
| Gas vehicles ≤ 8 model years old | Yes (pay $25 smog abatement fee) | No (transfer test required) |
| Gas vehicles ≤ 4 model years old | Yes | Yes |
| Electric vehicles | Yes | Yes |
| Diesel pre-1998 or >14,000 lbs GVWR | Yes | Yes |
| Motorcycles | Yes | Yes |
| **Collector motor vehicle, 35+ years old** | **Partial** (exempt from tailpipe only, must pass visual + fuel cap) | **Partial** |

### 1.4 The 1975 Cutoff — A Frozen Line

The 1975 model year cutoff was not always fixed. California previously had a **30-year rolling exemption** (established by SB 42 in 1997, signed by Governor Pete Wilson). Under this system, as each model year turned 30, it became smog-exempt.

In 2004, **AB 2683** (Lieber) repealed the rolling exemption and froze the cutoff at 1975. Governor Schwarzenegger signed it. The rationale: older vehicles are disproportionate polluters, and the rolling exemption was removing too many vehicles from the testing pool each year.

**Key consequence**: Every vehicle from 1976 onward is subject to smog testing in perpetuity. A 1990 Porsche 964 in 2026 still must pass smog — a 36-year-old car tested to the same OBD2 standard as a 2020 Camry.

### 1.5 New in 2025: Stricter Readiness Monitor Rules

As of October 1, 2025, BAR requires **all OBD2 readiness monitors to be set** for a vehicle to pass. Previously, certain hard-to-set monitors were exempted. This change makes it harder for older vehicles with marginal emissions systems to pass — they can no longer rely on monitor exemptions to hide degraded components.

**Impact on LS430**: A 2004-2006 LS430 with marginal catalytic converters may have previously passed because the cat efficiency monitor wasn't fully set. Under the new rules, the monitor must complete — and if the cat is degraded, it will flag P0420/P0430.

## 2. Leno's Law: History, Text, Status, and Prospects

### 2.1 Origin and Name

"Leno's Law" is named after Jay Leno, the former Tonight Show host and legendary car collector who has publicly championed smog reform for California's classic and collector vehicles. Leno has argued that collector vehicles represent less than 1% of vehicle miles traveled and that the smog check burden falls disproportionately on preservationists.

### 2.2 SB 712 (2025-2026 Session) — First Attempt

| Detail | Value |
|---|---|
| **Bill number** | SB 712 |
| **Author** | Senator Shannon Grove (R-Bakersfield) |
| **Sponsor** | Jay Leno; supported by SEMA |
| **Introduced** | Early 2025 |
| **Core provision** | 35-year rolling smog exemption for collector motor vehicles |
| **Original text** | Fully exempt collector vehicles ≥35 model years old from smog check, if insured as collector motor vehicle |
| **Amended text (March 2025)** | Removed collector-car insurance requirement; exempted vehicles 35+ years old regardless of insurance type |
| **Further amendments** | Added back requirements: historical vehicle plates, collector insurance, pre-1981 model year base |
| **Amended text (final version)** | Starting Jan 1, 2027: exempt pre-1981 vehicles with historical plates + collector insurance. Starting Jan 1, 2028: expand by one model year per year for 5 years (reaching pre-1986 by 2032) |
| **Senate vote** | Passed 32-3 with 5 not voting |
| **Assembly Transportation** | Passed |
| **Assembly Appropriations** | **Held in committee** — effectively killed for the 2025 session |
| **Final status** | Dead. August 29, 2025: "Held in committee and under submission" |

**Why SB 712 died**: The Assembly Appropriations Committee expressed concerns about:
- Fiscal impact on smog check revenue
- Emissions impact — even collector vehicles produce pollutants
- Regulatory precedent — opening the door to broader exemptions
- The definition of "collector motor vehicle" was initially too loose

### 2.3 SB 1392 (2025-2026 Session) — Second Attempt

| Detail | Value |
|---|---|
| **Bill number** | SB 1392 |
| **Author** | Senator Dave Cortese (D-San José), Chair of Senate Transportation Committee |
| **Principal co-author** | Senator Shannon Grove (R-Bakersfield) — bipartisan |
| **Sponsor** | Jay Leno; SEMA |
| **Introduced** | February 20, 2026 |
| **Core provision** | Phased smog check exemption for collector motor vehicles, starting pre-1981, rolling to pre-1986 by 2032 |
| **Key difference from SB 712** | New definition of "collector motor vehicle" in Vehicle Code §259; focus on vehicle use (not daily driver) rather than owner intent; requires collector vehicle insurance with proof at registration |
| **Phased rollout** | 2028: pre-1981 exempt → 2029: pre-1982 → 2030: pre-1983 → 2031: pre-1984 → 2032: pre-1986 |
| **Status (June 2026)** | Passed Senate Transportation Committee with bipartisan support. In Assembly. |
| **SEMA position** | Strongly supports — SEMA's Victor Muñoz coordinating grassroots campaign |

### 2.4 What SB 1392 Actually Does

1. **Exempts qualifying collector vehicles** from the requirement to obtain a certificate of compliance or noncompliance — both biennially and at transfer
2. **Defines "collector motor vehicle"** in VC §259 to mean a vehicle that:
   - Is not the owner's primary mode of transportation
   - Is maintained for use in shows, parades, charitable functions, and historical exhibitions
   - Has collector vehicle insurance
3. **Phases in** starting with pre-1981 MY vehicles in 2028, adding one model year annually through 2032
4. **Does NOT** eliminate emissions standards for newer vehicles or create a loophole for modified late-model cars
5. **Does NOT** create a true rolling exemption — it's a fixed expansion stopping at pre-1986

### 2.5 Critical Gap: SB 1392 Stops at Pre-1986

This is the most important detail for our analysis. **SB 1392, even if fully enacted, only exempts vehicles through the 1985 model year by 2032.** There is no mechanism for vehicles beyond 1985 to gain exemption.

The LS430 (2001-2006) is **16-21 model years beyond the 1985 cutoff**. SB 1392, as written, does NOT help the LS430.

A true rolling exemption (where the cutoff advances one year every year indefinitely) is what would eventually reach the LS430. Neither SB 712 (even in its original form) nor SB 1392 creates such a mechanism.

### 2.6 Prospects Assessment

| Factor | Assessment |
|---|---|
| **Bipartisan support** | Strong — Cortese (D) + Grove (R) is a powerful coalition |
| **SEMA lobbying** | Heavy — $40B industry, 149,000 jobs in CA |
| **Jay Leno celebrity factor** | Significant — media coverage, cultural cachet |
| **Environmental opposition** | Present but muted for pre-1986 vehicles |
| **Assembly Appropriations concern** | SB 712's death came here; SB 1392 addresses prior objections |
| **Governor's office** | Newsom has not signaled position; generally pro-environment |
| **Probability of passage (SB 1392)** | **40-55%** — better than SB 712, but not certain |
| **Probability of true rolling exemption (35-year)** | **10-20%** — would require separate future legislation |
| **Timeline for any LS430-relevant exemption** | **2040s at the earliest**, requiring multiple additional bills |

## 3. Scenario Modeling

### Scenario A: Status Quo — Nothing Passes

**Assumption**: SB 1392 fails. No smog exemption reform in the 2025-2026 or subsequent sessions. The 1975 cutoff remains frozen indefinitely.

**Implications**:
- All 1976+ vehicles subject to smog testing in perpetuity
- As vehicles age, their emissions systems degrade, making compliance harder and more expensive
- Pre-1976 vehicles command an increasing "smog-exempt premium" in California
- Cars like the LS430 (2001-2006) face escalating smog compliance costs as catalytic converters, O2 sensors, and EVAP components age
- The smog liability becomes a permanent drag on values of desirable but aging vehicles

**LS430 smog trajectory (Status Quo)**:
| Year | LS430 Age | Smog Risk | Estimated Cat Replacement Probability | Cost If Failed |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 2026 | 20-25 yrs | Moderate-High | 25-40% per biennial cycle | $4,000-6,000 |
| 2031 | 25-30 yrs | High | 40-60% | $4,500-6,500 (parts inflation) |
| 2036 | 30-35 yrs | Very High | 60-80% | $5,000-7,000 |
| 2041 | 35-40 yrs | Extreme | 80-95% | $5,500-8,000 |

### Scenario B: SB 1392 Passes (2026-2027) — Fixed Expansion to Pre-1986

**Assumption**: SB 1392 passes and is signed into law. Pre-1986 vehicles gain exemption by 2032. No further expansion.

**Implications for LS430**:
- **Direct impact: ZERO.** The LS430 is a 2001-2006 vehicle. SB 1392 stops at 1985.
- **Indirect impact: Slight positive.** Demonstrates legislative appetite for smog reform. Opens the door for future expansion bills.
- **Market psychology**: Collector car community sees momentum. Speculators may begin pricing in *anticipated* future exemptions for newer vehicles.

**Implications for pre-1986 vehicles**:
- Porsche 964 (1989-1994): Not covered (too new). Only the earliest 964s are close.
- Mercedes R107 (1971-1989): Pre-1981 R107s (450 SL, 280 SL early) gain exemption in 2028. Post-1980 R107s (380 SL, 500 SL) gain exemption 2029-2032.
- BMW E30 (1984-1991): Only pre-1986 E30s covered.
- Values of newly-exempt vehicles likely jump 10-20% as California-specific compliance costs vanish.

### Scenario C: True 35-Year Rolling Exemption Passes (Hypothetical)

**Assumption**: Future legislation (beyond SB 1392) establishes a true 35-year rolling exemption — every year, the exemption advances by one model year, indefinitely.

**LS430 exemption timeline under 35-year rolling**:

| LS430 Model Year | Year It Turns 35 | Smog Exempt In... |
|---|---|---|
| 2001 (first year) | 2036 | 2036 |
| 2004 (peak production) | 2039 | 2039 |
| 2006 (final year) | 2041 | 2041 |

**If this scenario materialized, the LS430 would become smog-exempt between 2036 and 2041.** This is the scenario that would most dramatically affect LS430 values.

|---|---|---|---|
| LS430 directly exempt? | No | No | Yes (2036-2041) |

## 4. LS430 Value Impact Under Each Scenario

### 4.1 Current LS430 Market (2026)

- Clean examples: $8,000-$12,000 (appreciating from ~$6,000 bottom in 2022)
- High-mileage (180K+): $5,000-$8,000
- Ultra-low-mileage (<50K): $15,000-$20,000+
- Annual appreciation: ~5-8% for clean driver-quality cars
- Key value drivers: reliability reputation, Q-factor (92.3 — highest in MotorGeek database), "last overengineered Lexus" narrative, cult following

### 4.2 The Smog Liability Discount

The LS430's hidden $4,000-6,000 smog liability (catalytic converter replacement) represents **33-50% of the car's value at $10-12K**. This is an enormous hidden cost that suppresses prices in California specifically.

In states without smog testing (or with 25-year exemptions), the LS430 faces no such liability. This creates a **California price discount** that is already observable — LS430s in smog-free states trade at modest premiums to California examples.

**Estimated California discount**: $1,000-3,000 on average (buyers discount for smog risk).

### 4.4 The "Exemption Premium" — What History Tells Us

Based on collector car market data (detailed in Section 6), newly-exempt vehicles typically see:

| Market Phase | Price Effect | Duration |
|---|---|---|
| Pre-exemption speculation (1-3 years before) | +5-15% | Speculators buy ahead of exemption |
| Exemption year | +10-25% | Demand spike as compliance costs vanish |
| Post-exemption settling (1-2 years) | -5-10% | Some profit-taking, prices stabilize |
| Long-term (3+ years) | New, higher floor | Exemption baked into baseline value |

For the LS430, if a 35-year rolling exemption were enacted with the LS430 becoming exempt in 2036:
- **2033-2035**: Speculation builds. Prices rise 10-20% above status quo.
- **2036-2041**: Exemption premium in full effect. Values 25-40% above status quo.
- **2042+**: New normal. The "smog-free LS430" is permanently more valuable than it would have been.

## 5. G90 5.0 Tau Implications

The G90 Tau is a 21st-century car with 21st-century emissions systems. Even under the most optimistic legislative scenario, it won't see exemption for 25-30 years. By then, the car will be a 45-year-old orphan — if any are still running.

### 5.2 Smog Risk Comparison: LS430 vs G90 Tau

| Factor | LS430 (2004-2006) | G90 5.0 Tau (2017-2020) |

| Age in 2026 | 20-25 years | 6-9 years |

### 5.3 The Tau's Advantage: Time

The G90 Tau's youth is its smog advantage. At 6-9 years old, its catalytic converters are healthy, its O2 sensors are fresh, and its readiness monitors set easily. The car will pass California smog without concern for another decade minimum.

The LS430's age is its smog disadvantage. At 20-25 years old, the cats are in the failure zone, O2 sensors are aged, and the new 2025 readiness monitor rules make marginal passes harder.

**Net effect**: The G90 Tau's ~$15K price premium over the LS430 is partially justified by its ~$0 smog risk vs. the LS430's ~$2-4K expected smog liability over a 5-year ownership period.

## 6. Market Parallels: Porsche, Mercedes, BMW

### 6.1 California's Previous Rolling Exemption (1997-2004)

When California had a 30-year rolling exemption (established 1997, repealed 2004), the effect on newly-exempt vehicles was measurable:

- **1974 model year vehicles** became exempt in 2004 (the year the repeal took effect). Values for desirable 1974 models (Porsche 914, Mercedes 450 SL, early BMW 2002s) saw a noticeable bump in California markets.
- **1975 model year** would have been exempt in 2005, but the repeal froze the line. Cars that *almost* became exempt (1976-1980 models) were permanently trapped.
- The 1976+ trap created a permanent value gap in California: a 1975 Porsche 911 Carrera is worth measurably more than a 1976, partly because one is smog-exempt and the other is not.

### 6.2 Porsche 964/993: The Air-Cooled 911 Explosion

The Porsche 993 (1994-1998 US) is the best available parallel for what happens when a desirable car gains collector status:

| Year | 993 Carrera Coupe Value (#3 Good Condition) | Key Event |
|---|---|---|
| 2005 | ~$20,000 | Just a used car |
| 2010 | ~$25,000 | "Last air-cooled" narrative begins |
| 2014 | ~$35,000 | Collector market heating up |
| 2017 | ~$55,000 | Peak frenzy; Hagerty #3 hits $57K |
| 2019 | ~$45,000 | Correction; values settle |
| 2022 | ~$50,000 | Pandemic bump |
| 2025 | ~$55-60,000 | Stabilized at new floor |

**Key dynamics**:
- The 993 roughly **tripled** from bottom ($20K) to peak ($55-60K) over ~15 years
- The "last air-cooled" narrative drove premium, not smog exemption (993s are still smog-tested in CA)
- After the initial frenzy, values corrected ~20%, then stabilized at a new floor roughly 2.5x the bottom
- The most desirable variants (Turbo, C4S, RS) appreciated far more than base Carreras

**LS430 parallel**: The LS430 has its own "last overengineered Lexus" narrative, but lacks the brand cachet and motorsport heritage of the 911. A 2-3x return over 15 years (to $25-30K) is plausible under favorable conditions; the 3-4x seen by the 993 is the ceiling, requiring both exemption passage AND broader market recognition.

### 6.3 Mercedes R107 SL: Slow, Steady Appreciation

The Mercedes R107 SL (1971-1989) is a better parallel for the LS430's likely trajectory:

| Era | R107 Value Range | Narrative |
|---|---|---|
| 1990s-2000s | $5,000-12,000 | Depreciated luxury, "old man's car" |
| 2010-2015 | $10,000-20,000 | "Youngtimer" recognition begins |
| 2016-2020 | $15,000-30,000 | Collector interest, Hagerty #2 values $30-45K |
| 2021-2026 | $15,000-35,000 | Stable, slow appreciation for best examples |

**Key dynamics**:
- The R107 appreciated slowly and steadily — no wild spikes
- Early pre-1981 models gained a California smog premium (~$2-5K) vs. post-1980 models
- Production numbers were high (~237,000 over 18 years) — similar to the LS430 (~160,000 over 5 years, US)
- The R107's value floor was driven by build quality and brand, not performance

**LS430 parallel**: The LS430's appreciation curve is likely more R107 than 993 — slow, steady, driven by quality recognition rather than hysteria. The smog exemption (if it comes) would add a step-change similar to what pre-1981 R107s saw.

### 6.4 BMW E30 M3: The Speculative Spike

The E30 M3 (1986-1991) saw a dramatic value explosion:

| Year | E30 M3 Value |
|---|---|
| 2005 | $8,000-12,000 |
| 2012 | $20,000-30,000 |
| 2016 | $50,000-70,000 |
| 2020 | $60,000-80,000 |
| 2025 | $55,000-75,000 |

The E30 M3's spike was driven by rarity (~5,000 US units), motorsport heritage, and the "first M3" narrative. The LS430 lacks both rarity and motorsport heritage, making the E30 M3's trajectory an upper-bound reference, not a likely path.

### 6.5 Market Parallel Summary

| Parallel | Price Multiplier (Bottom to Peak) | Time to Peak | Applicability to LS430 |
|---|---|---|---|
| Porsche 993 | 2.5-3.0x | 12-15 years | Medium — strong narrative, but 911 has more brand power |

| BMW E30 M3 | 6-8x | 10-12 years | Low — too rare, too special |
| Porsche 964 | 2.5-3.5x | 10-15 years | Medium — "last of" narrative similar to LS430 |

**Most likely LS430 trajectory** (Scenario A, status quo): **1.8-2.5x over 15 years**, reaching $18-25K by 2041. Slow, steady appreciation driven by quality recognition, reliability reputation, and "last overengineered" narrative. Smog liability partially offsets gains.

**With exemption** (Scenario C): **2.5-4.0x over 15 years**, reaching $25-40K by 2041. Exemption removes the smog discount, adds a speculative premium, and unlocks the full collector car market (buyers currently avoiding CA smog risk re-enter).
